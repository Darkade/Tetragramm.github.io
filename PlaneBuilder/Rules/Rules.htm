These are the rules you�ll use to create airplanes. This is an involved and complex project, but you can do it!

Plane Building Stats

Here�s a list of the Stats that matter for airplanes as you build.

 

Input Stats

-          Mass: Mass is how much stuff weighs. Keeping your mass low is a priority, but engines get a lot bigger as they get more powerful, so it matters less and less over time.

-          Drag: Drag how much stuff catches airflow, slowing the plane down. Lower is better.

-          Structure: Used to determine how tough an airplane is overall. Structure is used as a cap for Maximum Strain and will eventually become the Structural Integrity stat. Higher is better.

-          Stability: Used to determine how stable the aircraft is in flight. It is divided into two stats, which will be synthesized at the end to create the final Stability stat. Less or more is dependent on what you want the aircraft to do.

-          Lateral Stability: Combines Yaw and Roll stability. Usually lower, especially on planes with high torque.

-          Pitch Stability: Usually the higher of the two, this stat is more easily changed.

-          Control: How much control the aircraft has. More is better.

-          Cost: How much money everything costs. More costs more.

-          Sections: How physically large the aircraft�s hull is. More means a larger, heavier aircraft.

-          Wing Area: How large a wing is. Larger induces more drag but also lifts heavier planes.

-          Wing Span: How long a wing is. Longer is more efficient for drag and more stable, but more fragile and lowers Control.

-          Lift Bleed: How efficient the wings are. Lower is better.

-          Power: How much go the engine has.

 

Output Stats

-          Top Speed: How fast the planes flies.

-          Boost: How fast the plane accelerates.

-          Dropoff: The point of speed at which your engine is more or less efficient.

-          Stall Speed: How slow the plane can go before it falls out of the sky.

-          Handling: How well the aircraft handles.

-          Control: How much Control the aircraft has over pitch specifically, for hard pull-ups.

-          Structural Integrity: How tough plane is. Basically HP. Divided into two stats.

-          Maximum Strain: How much G-Forces the plane can take.

-          Toughness: Excess health.

-          Visibility: How hard it is to see out of the plane.

-          Stability: How well the plane doesn�t accidently flip over.

-          Fuel Capacity: How much fuel the plane carries.

-          Stress per Flight: How hard the plane is to fly for a while.

-          Safety Stats

-          Crash Safety: How safe it is to crash land.

-          Escape: How easy it is to get out in a hurry.

-          Upkeep: How much the aircraft costs per routine (standard game only).

 

Basics

Planes are built out of components. We�re going to build it one part at a time in the following order.

-          Cockpits: Defining who is inside the aircraft.

-          Engine: Defining how the aircraft is powered and how it is cooled.

-          Weapons: What the aircraft is armed with.

-          Sections: Putting an actual frame around everything above.

-          Load: Fuel, bombs, and passengers.

-          Wings: Making it fly.

-          Stabilizers & Control: Making it steer.

-          Reinforcement: Making sure the aircraft stays together.

-          Extras: Everything else.

-          Final Stats: Putting it together into a profile you can use in game.

 

Your plane will generally need, at minimum, a pilot, an engine, and some wings. Without wings, it�s a car. Without an engine, it's a glider. Without a pilot, it�s a missile.

 

As you build the plane, remember planes cannot have negative Drag or Mass; if you manage to make one like that, you will always at least have 1 MP and 1 Drag.

Engines

Engines come in two general types, Pusher engines and Tractor engines. Tractor engines have the propeller ahead of the plane pulling it, while pushers have it behind the plane pushing it away.

Choosing your Engine

Your engine can be chosen from the list of premade engines in this book or any of the aircraft books.

 

Generally speaking, air cooled engines are lighter but less powerful while water cooled engines are heavier but more powerful.

Mounting your Engine

Engines can be mounted in a variety of ways.

 

A rear-mounted pusher represents an engine mounted at the far end of the aircraft�s body, like the pusher engines of a Kyushu J7W. A center-mounted pusher engine represents a pusher engine that still has a tail, using an extended driveshaft or Farman tail to avoid imbalance.

Engine Torque and Rotaries

Engines have Torque, which is subtracted from their Lateral Stability directly if it uses any hull mounts. Wing mounts minimize the effect of Torque, so ignore it there.

 

Rotary engines are usually the only engines that you need to worry about this with in early aircraft, seeing as they are a giant chunk of metal spinning at high speeds.

 

Wing mounted rotary engines reduce your Strain by the Torque (use all engines), representing the force against the wing. In a push-pull configuration, you can choose to reduce Structure instead.

Cooling (Liquid)

If your engine is liquid-cooled, you�ll need to add a radiator. A radiator weighs 3 Mass and has a variable amount of Drag, which you choose. You cannot have more radiators than you have engines, but you can opt to connect multiple engines to the same enlarged radiator. This�ll save weight, but be a single point of failure.

 

The Drag of a radiator is how much of the surface of the radiator is exposed to the air. Every point of Drag gives +2 Cooling. You must have a Cooling valuable equal to the Cooling of your engine(s) to get the Radiator�s value. Every point of radiator you have less than that gives -1 Reliability. Having more Cooling than your Engine requires will not make it more reliable.

 

The Type is the design of the radiator, and the Mount of the radiator is where it is in relation to the engine (not the aircraft as a whole) and it affects radiator performance after it gets shot. Both of these can give straight reliability bonuses to the engine due to mechanical simplicity or efficiency.

 

Radiators can have different types. If the radiator gains more Drag, it will not add more Reliability: this is from the other parts of the design like hoses or framework.

Evaporative Cooling

An alternate way to cool a liquid cooled engine is evaporative cooling. This requires a sufficiently large wing (at least 5 Area per engine) with some sort of metal skin. Rather than running water to a radiator, the water is sprayed as steam into a cavity in the wings to cool it, where it is collected and fed back into the engine. This is more streamlined, but is very fragile.

 

Evaporative cooling only costs the +3 Mass you�d pay to get a radiator. There�s no Drag involved, you�re just ready to go.

 

The downside is that any attack with a Crit roll of 16+ will knock out the radiator, in addition to any other damage done or other parts damaged.

Weapon Sizes

Weapons come in different Sizes: Tiny, Light, Medium, Heavy, and Artillery.

 

You can always mount smaller weapons in areas with restrictions, like turrets or wings. You can mount twice as many weapons of one size smaller than a larger weapon. For example, on a turret with a Medium weapon mount, you could mount 2 Light Weapons.

Systems

Weapons of the same type which fire in the same direction form a in System.� All their hits are added together to create their weapon profiles, creating a number of hits and an amount of damage done at the four range bands of Knife/Close/Long/Extreme.

 

The aircraft weapons listed below are mounted into aircraft as part of a �system� of identical weapons. It doesn�t matter where each weapon in the system is mounted so long as they all fire at the same place at once.

 

To create the range chart, add together all the Hits from the guns, then multiply it by the weapon�s damage to get your damage at that range.

 

Weapon Hits drop off with range. Centerline and turret weapons have a dropoff of 100/75/50/25 percent, rounding down, for each range band in sequence. For wing mounted guns, use 80/100/30/10 percent instead. If you�ve got a mix of centerline and wing guns, calculate the two groups separately, then add them together.

Placement

You may place your weapons on the hull or the wings of the aircraft. Any number of weapons can be placed on or in the hull of the aircraft, though note that Artillery-sized weapons must each have their own frame section.

 

Wing mounted weapons are restricted based on the strength of the wing�s construction, as follows.

-          No cantilevers: Max 2 Light weapons only.

-          Wood or bone cantilevers: Max 2 Medium weapons only.

-          Metal cantilevers: Max 2 Heavy weapons.

 

Mounting a weapon heavier than this on the wing will cost +2 Mass, representing extending support beams from the hull of the plane, in the manner of the Becker 20mm cannon on the Albatros D.II.

 

We can imagine these weapons being mounted atop the upper wing, out along the length of the wing, and so forth.

 

For airplanes with traditional tractor configurations, any placement outside of the arc of the propeller is considered a wing placement.

 

An Artillery weapon mounted fixed forward will interfere with an engine. You must either use nacelles, a pusher, or a center-mount tractor engine with the appropriate modifications. Artillery weapons can never be mounted on wings.

When a weapon is wing mounted and uncovered, it has +1 Drag.

 

Covered
Weapons may be covered or uncovered.

 

When you add weapons to your airplane, they start as uncovered.

Uncovered weapons add the drag listed in the weapon's description to the aircraft.

For the cost of 1 unit of mass, you may upgrade a weapon (or pair of weapons) to covered, this eliminates its drag. This may involve moving the weapons to inside the hull or wings of the aircraft, or it may just involve building aerodynamic fairings over them.

Weapons mounted on wings always have 1 drag, even when covered.

Accessibility
Weapons jam. Some need to be manually reloaded. Weapons that can have a crew member operate on them to carry out these sorts of activities are called accessible. This may involve making it so you can reach the weapon, such as a foster mount that lets you pull a gun to your cockpit, or it may involve hydraulic systems to charge guns and clear jams.

 

Weapon systems are accessible or inaccessible as a group. You never have a situation where one weapon can be operated by the other can�t.

One weapon (or pair of weapons) placed on the hull can always be freely accessible per crew station. Which particular weapon is accessible from which crew station must be decided when you place the weapons. Even when covered, it wasn�t uncommon for the backs of these weapons to protrude into the cockpit area, or for a simple mechanical plunger to be used to clear jams.

Any weapons placed on the wings and any more weapons placed on the hull start as inaccessible.

Covered weapons mounted in a turret (see below) start as accessible if uncovered weapons in the same mount would be accessible.

It costs � equal to half the weapons in a group to make them all accessible, minimum 1.

Arcs & Mounting

We divide the arcs of fire of a weapon into the following directions.

 

Forward, Rearward, Up, Down, Left, Right

 

Fixed weapons fire in only one of these directions because they are bolted directly to the aircraft�s frame. This direction will probably be Forward, unless you are doing something very clever.

 

Flexible weapons can be changed to fire in more than one direction, having different settings for which direction they face without being moved and fired at the same time.� Flexible weapons cost 1� and that gives you two directions the weapon can be pointed.

 

Turret weapons are any weapon which is flexibly mounted to a spot. This costs 1� to set up, and encompasses an entire system aimed by the gunner. Pick two directions for the weapon to fire. Expanding a turret�s firing arc costs 1� for 2 additional directions, then 1� each.

 

A Turret initially has capacity for a single Light weapon (or two Tiny weapons). They can be upgraded to a Medium weapon for 1�, a Heavy weapon slot for 1 Mass and 3�, and an Artillery weapon for 2 Mass and 5�. As usual, you can mount two of any weapon one size smaller than the capacity in the turret.

 

Finally, you can install Weapon Braces inside a cockpit. This are basically clips or mounts a weapon can be attached to to fire in any direction. 3 directions worth of Bracing costs 1�. These mounts will allow an observer a +1 to fire a loose weapon they are carrying in their hands. The downside is that this is often very dangerous, and will prompt frequent Wingwalking moves. Remember: you still have to pay the mass and drag cost of the weapon that will go on these braces.

Synchronization

If you wish to fire through the arc of a propeller (ahead of you with a tractor plane or behind you with a pusher plane), you�ll need to make provisions to avoid hitting the propeller blades. This is done with Synchronization. To represent the different qualities of Synchronization options, we use the following options.

 

-          An Interrupter Gear represents the early versions, and cap weapons at 3 hits. 2� per gun.

-          A Synchronization Gear represents more sophisticated later systems, and do not cause caps. 3� per gun.

 

A Sync/Interrupter Gear can only be mounted on a weapon that allows it to be synchronized, and will only work if the weapon is also Fixed.

 

If you are cheap, you can substitute this for Deflector Plates. This costs 1� and will work, but it inflicts 1 Wear every time you roll a natural 5 or less!

Spinner Weapons

If you have a Geared Propeller and either a liquid-cooled engine with at least two banks or a radial (not rotary) engine with six or fewer cylinders, a single (not a pair) weapon of Heavy or smaller size can be mounted such that it fires through the center of the propeller. This represents things like the centreline 37mm on the SPAD S.XII.

 

Spinner weapons bypass the need for synchronizers. They cannot be turrets.

Monocoque

You can build a monocoque (one-shell) or semi-monocoque plane if you wish.

 

A monocoque or semi-monocoque plane can be built by substituting the mass and structural bonus from a piece of frame for a monocoque-compatible skin piece. The frame still exists: few monocoque aircraft are truly without a frame, these frameworks are just minimized and incorporated into the shell structure.

 

Monocoque skin pieces cost +1� each, representing the labour cost of designing and building it. This is in addition to the frame cost: that doesn�t go away.

Lifting Body & Flying Wing

A lifting body and flying wings are both incredibly complicated engineering achievements and require the aircraft to have a solid skin (Molded plywood or better).

 

A Lifting Body aircraft counts each Frame Piece as being 3m2 of wing area for the purposes of calculating stall speed, and adds +1 Drag per piece.

 

A Flying Wing is a Lifting Body that also takes +5 Lift Bleed to represent the overly thick wing chord. It eliminates all Drag generated by each Frame Piece, including the extra Drag for being a Lifting Body, in exchange.[1] 

 

Both these aircraft still have tails, even if they are blended into the rest of the machine.

Internal Bracing

To increase the resilience of an aircraft, you can add Internal Bracing. This is basically extra Frame Pieces that you don�t have to put Skin on, because it is on the inside. You can have 1 Internal Bracing piece per actual frame section. They do not have to be the same material as everything else: you can build a wooden aircraft with some steel bracing, for example.

 

Titanium can only be used for internal bracing. Making a whole plane out of Titanium is like making an entire ring out of diamonds: cool, but way too expensive to be worth it.

Wings

You need wings to fly, or something wing-like at least. A greater wing area means more lift on your plane, but also means more drag and less structural integrity.

 

To start with, your airplane�s wings have a number called their Lift Bleed, determined by your Era. This represents how much lift is lost to inefficiency in wing design. Ideally, this number would be 1.

Wing Area

Start by deciding how much wing area you want your plane to have overall, across all the wings. Write it down as Meters Squared.

 

For every Area, gain +1 Drag.

 

Now, divide this area up into each wing plane your aircraft will have. Each wing added after the first gives�

-          �+3 Control

-          +5 Lift Bleed

-          -1 Visibility

Aspect Ratio

Wings are not all the same shape; some wings are long and skinny (they have a high aspect ratio) and some wings are short and wide (they have a low aspect ratio).

 

Decide the wingspan of each wing on the plane, alongside the area you�ve given it.

 

Wings have a strain modifier and additional Drag.

 

Strain reduction = 2Span + Area - 10.

 

Extra Drag is Area - Span. This can result in reduced overall drag from the wing, but any given wing must always generate at least 1 Drag.

 

The Fokker E.III has a wing area of 16 square meters and a wingspan of 9 meters. This means the wing area generates 16 Drag, and the wingspan adds +7 Drag, for a total of 23.

 

A theoretical glider has 10 square meters of wings and a 15m wingspan. It generates 10 drag for its area, but because the wings are 5 meters long than the area, it loses 5 drag as well. Thus, it generates just 5 Drag total, and inflicts a -30 Strain Penalty.

Longest Wing Effects

The longest wing on your aircraft will give the following modifier.

 

-          8 - Wingspan = Control Modifier

-          Every point of Wingspan less than 8: -1 Lateral Stability.

-          Every multiple of the Frame + Tail Slots in meters after the first: +1 Lateral Stability

Closed Wing

Creating a closed wing, such as a box or circular structure, eliminates the vortex effect on the end of a wing, reducing drag, and also allows a more complete structure. On the other hand, this design will be difficult to control, due to the added weight on the extreme ends of the wings increasing the energy required to induce a roll.

 

A closed wing costs +1 Mass, -5 Control, and +20 Max Strain.

 

A closed monowing isn�t possible: wingtip loops to reduce drag would be part of optimization.

Inline Wings

A style of Tandem (or Semi-Tandem) Wing where both wings are on the same deck level. Reduces the Drag of the trailing wing equal to half the area of the leading wing. Remember a wing always needs to have at least 1 Drag. +2 Lift Bleed.

Miniature Wings

A Wing of 2 or less square meters is a Miniature Wing. These effectively do not count as a wing: they do not add the effects of whatever wing deck they are added onto, and instead just add +1 Control and their size for lift and strain purposes. Each Miniature Wings past the first add +1 Lift Bleed each.

 

Each miniature wing must be mounted on their own deck: they can�t occupy the same space as another wing. No tandem miniature wings!

Wing Angle & Gull Wings

Wings can be built at angles to change their properties. Wingtips up (dihedral wings) improve stability, as they make the plane more likely to roll back to a neutral position. Anhedral wings do the opposite.

 

Inducing a dihedral angle on the wing will add Lateral Stability, while an anhedral wing will remove Lateral Stability instead. In either case, your total Wing Bleed increases by the amount of stability added or lost.

 

Gull wings are Dihedral or Anhedral wings that also reduce effective wingspan for Longest Wing effects, at a rate of 1 meter per 2 Lift Bleed gained, rounding up. This also physically shortens the wings, which is useful if you have a constraint of wingspan for whatever reason (like carrier planes).

Swept Wings

Swept Wings add +5 Lift Bleed and give -1 Lateral Stability. However, they allow the complete elimination of the horizontal stabilizer and give a natural mounting point for Outboard Vertical Stabilizers.

Special Wing Types

There are a number of special wing types. These wing types can be combined with normal wings, but it will rarely be worth it.

-          Gyrocopters use a rotating wing surface. They are very stable for light aircraft.

-          Helicopters power a rotating wing surface, allowing free vertical and horizontal movement at the cost of low stability and requiring a powerful engine.

-          Ornithopters use an engine to power a flapping effect. This creates supremely maneuverable but delicate and expensive craft.

-          Electric lifters use an exposed high voltage wire to create a downward rush of air through ionization. They are power intensive but allow variable lift.

-          Rigid and non-rigid airships use lighter-than-air gases to achieve lift.

 

They are detailed in the next chapter.

 

Stabilizers

An aircraft must be made stable. It must have a horizontal stabilizer to keep the nose pointing flat, and a vertical stabilizer to prevent spinning and rolling. You can design a plane without these things, but it�s really hard.

Stabilizer Size

Airplanes need stabilizers to fly. Full stop. If you don�t have them and you haven�t done something very, very clever, your plane does a wibbly sort of motion and goes into the ground.

 

Your Stabilizers cost Drag, representing them getting in the airstream.

 

-          All your vertical stabilizers will add 1/10th the total Wing Area in Drag. (Minimum 1)

-          All your horizontal stabilizers will add 1/5th the total Wing Area in Drag. (Minimum 1)

Load

The Load is everything that goes atop the aircraft after it is complete. This is where the difference between the Dry Mass (the aircraft as it is) and the Wet Mass (the aircraft with fuel and bombs) comes from.

 

Wet Mass is essentially separate mass, and it is always rounded up to the nearest MP, unlike with usual calculations. So if you have a plane that comes in at 41 Dry Mass (8MP), you can�t put 3 fuel into it and stay at 8MP. Fuel only comes in 1MP chunks in the regular tanks. 1 Mass of bombs or 5 all counts as 1 MP, so if you can, always take an even MP of bombs.

 

This is for the sake of simplifying book-keeping at the table, and ensuring that fuel always has a penalty associated with it. �

Fuel

Engines have Fuel Consumption, which uses an abstracted unit of fuel. Each Mass of fuel is 25 of those points. So basically, multiply the amount of fuel mass by 25, then divided by the consumption of all your engines, and that�s how many fuel uses you have.

 

You can fit 2 Fuel Tanks into 1 aircraft section, or add Fuel Tanks onto a wing for +3 Drag each. If you have a Cantilever wing, you can put 1 Fuel Tank per 10 Area into the wings with no additional drag instead.

 

A fuel tank gives you up to 5 mass of Fuel. The tank itself weighs 1 Mass.

 

A Micro-Tank is a 1 Mass tank that holds 1 Mass of fuel and does not use a frame slot.

Bombs

Aircraft can, if set up for it, carry bombs. Exactly how that ends up getting filled out is up to you and your loadout at the time.

 

An External Bomb Mount that can carry up to 5 Mass of bombs costs 1 Mass and does not take up a frame slot. An internal bomb slot takes up a hull section.

 

An Internal Bomb Bay takes up a frame slot, and can carry up to half the aircraft�s total bomb capacity. With two bomb bays, all your munitions can be carried internally.

 

Regardless, aircraft are limited to carrying, total, no more than 1/5th their total Structure in Mass of munitions.

 

Bomb masses are always rounded up to the closest Mass Penalty; 1 Mass of bombs is still treated as 1 Mass Penalty.

Final Calculations

Mass

Add all your points of mass together. Your Mass Penalty (MP) is 1/5th of that (rounding down) Do not include the mass of your Fuel or Bombs (this will be your Dry Weight).

Keep your MP low so you can climb more easily and maneuver.

 

Drag

Add your Mass Penalty (MP) directly to your Drag. Then add all your Drag points together. Your Drag Penalty (DP) is 1/5th of that (rounding down).

 

Keep your DP low to travel more quickly.

Multiply your Max Speed by your Pitch Speed Mod.

 

Your Boost is the Power of your engine(s) divided by your Mass, rounded down. If you get 0, you need more power to overcome resting friction, so get bigger engines or lose some weight.

 

Your Dropoff is your final Max Speed multiplied by your Pitch Boost Mod.

 

Stall Speed

To get your stall speed, multiply Lift Bleed by Mass Penalty, and divide by Wing Area.

 

Stability

Just add your two Stabilities together to get your value. However, if both your Stabilities are positive, add an additional +2 Stability, and do the opposite if they are both negative. Planes need to be a little unstable to fly, but too unstable and they don�t really fly.

Handling & Elevators

Handling = 100 + Control - Mass Penalty

 

Your Elevators stat is Handling / 10, rounding down, minimum 1.

 

Energy Loss vs Turn Bleed

These two stats are how much speed you lose when you pull up, and how much speed you lose in a combat turn.

 

Your Turn Bleed is 1/6th your Stall Speed at half fuel rounded up.

 

Your Energy Loss is 1/6th your Drag Penalty rounded up.

 

If you carry bombs, make a note: your Turn Bleed will be +1 while carrying bombs.

 

If you carry external bombs, your Energy Loss will also be +1 until they�re gone.

 

Turn Bleed is minimum 1. Energy Loss is maximum 10.

 

Pilot Fatigue

The default Stress per flight is 1. You gain +1 Stress for each of the factors which is true.

-          A Stability above 3 or below -3.

-          A tractor rotary engine + open cockpit.

-          Every 10MP.

 

Upkeep

This stat is Engine Power divided by 10, or the total cost of all the engines, whichever is lower.

 

Max Strain, and Toughness

Your total Structure is just your Structure. No modifications are needed.

 

Subtract your MP from your Max Strain at the end. Your Max Strain is always capped to your total Structure.

 

Your Toughness is (Structure - Max Strain)/2. Then. add 1 Toughness per 5 Strain.

 

Even if your Strain was higher than your Structure: you always have at least a little Toughness from the second step, just to give you a little buffer.

 

Vital Parts

Determine Vital Parts list as follows.

 

-          All aircraft have the Controls vital part.

-          Each Weapon System on the aircraft is a Vital Part.

-          If the aircraft has fuel tanks, they are a Vital Part.

-          If the aircraft has any electrical systems at all, they all form one Vital Part called Electronics.

-          If the aircraft has any fuel tanks at all, they collectively form one Vital Part.

-          Each Engine is a vital part.

-          Each Radiator is a vital part.

-          Each Oil Tank is a vital part.

 

 

Double Plane

A cheap and easy way to make a heavy fighter is to glue two light fighters together. Just add all their stats together and recalculate. You can opt to remove the other cockpit to save 1 mass, but otherwise they both function.

 

The resulting plane can then be modified on their own. Technically, you could make a triple plane or more this way as well! But then it starts to get a little silly.

Home Engineering

As much as this system tries to be completely comprehensive, it can�t always be. Sometimes you want to go beyond the bounds of the system, or you think your character can do something clever. That�s where Home Engineering comes in.

Unwise Modification

Go to your GM and say �I have an idea for something I want to do to my plane.� Lay out the mechanics for how you envision this.

 

If this modification does not totally break the system, the GM will then charge you a fair price for it in Thaler, drag, mass, etc. They should always err on the cheaper side.

 

The modification has the effect you want, but there is a price. The GM gets to add a new Hard Move they can use against you, as follows.

Personal Optimization

As an alternate to the regular optimization process, which is presumed to involve hiring specialists, your character can try to do it themselves. If you try this, roll +Calm.

 

On a miss, you break something or spend a lot of money chasing a dead end. On a partial, you may choose one rank of any of the Optimizations, but the GM offers you a trade-off for it. Yes, you can be more streamlined, but it will cost mass. Yes, your engine can be more powerful, but it�ll lower your Max Strain, etc.

 

On a full success, you simply get the optimization.

 

Doing this always costs 1 Stress.
